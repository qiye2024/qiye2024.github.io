Publications

  • 2024.09.27
    Climate changes drive phytoplankton community through complementarity and selection effects in a large mesotrophic reservoir
    Journal of Oceanology and Limnology
    This study analyzes long-term data from Daxi Reservoir, showing that increasing precipitation and water temperature have led to stronger thermal stratification, lower nitrogen levels, and a more diverse and productive phytoplankton community. Despite stable nutrient levels, cyanobacteria have increased due to shifts in niche complementarity and selection effects, highlighting the need for stricter phosphorus control to mitigate future risks under climate change.
  • 2024.07.23
    Response of phytoplankton functional group to spring drought in a large subtropical reservoir
    Journal of Oceanology and Limnology
    This study examines the impact of drought on the aquatic environment and phytoplankton succession in Daxi Reservoir, revealing that lower water levels intensify sediment resuspension, increasing phosphorus-enriched turbidity and shifting phytoplankton composition toward diatoms adapted to turbid, nutrient-rich conditions. Given the challenges of nutrient control, timely water replenishment is recommended as a practical strategy to manage harmful algae and maintain reservoir ecological stability during droughts.
  • 2024.02.20
    Response of a submerged macrophyte (Vallisneria natans) to water depth gradients and sediment nutrient concentrations
    Science of The Total Environment
    This study examines how water depth and sediment nutrient levels influence the growth of Vallisneria natans, revealing that water depth has a stronger impact by inducing morphological and physiological adaptations to light stress. While the plant enhances root structure and photosynthetic efficiency to cope with environmental changes, excessive water depth and nutrient limitations can hinder its growth, highlighting implications for lake management and ecological restoration.
  • 2022.02.25
    Pangenomic and functional investigations for dormancy and biodegradation features of an organic pollutant-degrading bacterium Rhodococcus biphenylivorans TG9
    Science of The Total Environment
    This study investigates the relationship between dormancy and biodegradation in Rhodococcus biphenylivorans TG9, highlighting its ability to degrade various organic pollutants, including biphenyl and polychlorinated biphenyls. Genomic and functional analyses reveal key genes involved in dormancy, resuscitation, and degradation, providing insights into enhancing bioremediation strategies.
